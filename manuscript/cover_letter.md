Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"Engineering for stress does not exempt it: root-architecture
and transcriptomic response of AVP-OX cotton to spaceflight,"** for consideration as an Article in
*npj Microgravity*.

Whether crops engineered for terrestrial stress tolerance retain a benefit in spaceflight is an open and
practically important question for space agriculture. Using the TIC-TOC experiment (AVP1-overexpressing
cotton flown on the ISS, SpaceX CRS-22), we paired **time-series root-architecture imaging** with **root
and shoot transcriptomics** in a full factorial design, and analysed them through a reproducible pipeline
(DESeq2, ortholog-mapped GO/KEGG, PhysioSpace stress decoding, and WGCNA co-expression).

Our central finding is supported by four independent analyses: the spaceflight response is strongly
**tissue-specific**, and the AVP-OX engineering acts as an **attenuator** of the defence/stress
programme — suppressing jasmonate/wounding signalling and lowering PhysioSpace stress activation — **while
the engineered lines sustain a stronger primary-root spaceflight response than wild type.** In short,
AVP-OX cotton appears to experience microgravity as less stressful while growing more root — a favourable
phenotype for space agriculture. A co-expression module linking signalling/isoprenoid metabolism to the
growth phenotype nominates hormone-precursor metabolism as a testable mechanism.

We believe this work fits *npj Microgravity*'s scope in spaceflight plant biology and its emphasis on
open, reproducible science: all analysis code and derived data are openly available
(https://github.com/dr-richard-barker/TICTOC), with raw data archived at NASA OSDR/GeneLab. We report our
statistical limitations transparently (group-level transcriptome–architecture integration; image
calibration).

This manuscript is original, not under consideration elsewhere, and all authors approve submission. We
have no competing interests.
